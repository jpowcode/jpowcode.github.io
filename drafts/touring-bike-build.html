Touring Bike Build

Wed 19 October 2016
By jpowcode

I've started to do a spot of cycling touring. Obviously before doing this I would need to build a new touring bike. After many hours of internet research where I read many different opinions I decided on the following build. I will explain my reasoning.

Frame

A steel construction by a fantastic little shop specialising in touring bikes based in the Ambleside in the Lake District. I wanted steel as it is the only material that can be easily repaired by anyone with basic welding gear pretty much anywhere in the world. I particularly liked the Ghylside frame as the geometry is s lightly more akin to a normal road bike rather than a traditional tourer. I thought this might give a bit more of a lively ride on days when it's not fully laden. It has wide tire clearance for tires up to about 40mm so could be taken off road. All the braze on mounts are featured including mudguards and a third bottle cage.

Bearings

I've opted for sealed stainless steel bearings in the headset and the bottom bracket made by Hope. I know they wouldn't be easy to source in far flung places if they went wrong, but I figured they are likely to last for such a long time that this is unlikely anyway. The bearings could always be replaced before a long trip to minimise risk.

Gears

This took a good while to figure out. I wanted an 11 speed setup as my wife's touring bike is 11 speed and it would be helpful to have common spares on a trip. And then the problems began. I started with a compact chainset and a 32 tooth rear cassette. A standard Shimano 105 setup. I toured around some of the Scotish Islands for a week on this. By staying indoors I kept the load down to a single pair of rear panniers. This worked out ok, just! Some of the hills were a bit of a challenge. My next trip was going to involve camping and the addition of front panniers and more weight. Lower gears were needed. I considered a few options

1. Swap the chainset on the front to a triple. The only problem being that Shimano do not make a triple chainset in 11 speed so I would have to change the entire grouset back to a 10 speed.

2. Swap the chainset for one such as a Sugino where the chainrings can be swapped. This is compatible with a Shimano 11 speed groupset. The downside would be to get low enough gears I would have to sacrifice some higher gears.

3. Replace the cassette with an 11 speed mountain bike one with a 40 tooth maximum sprocket and change the derailleur for a moutain bike one. The only problem here is that Shimano road and mountain bike shifters pull different amount of cable through to change gear and wanted to keep the road bike shifters. This could be fixed with downtube friction shifters or barend shifters, but I like the paddle shifters on the gear leavers and didn't want to give them up.

4. Same as option 3 but make use of a Jtek gear shift pulley to change the amount of cable pull from the road shifters to make them compatible with the moutanin bike derailleur. These Jtek pulleys are available to make pretty much any mech compatible with any shifter. Shimano and capag for example.

In the end I opted for a option 4 with a Shimano XT 11-40 cassette and XT derailleur. I was a bit dubious that it would work but gave it go anyway. After setting it all up I was pleasantly surprised. The gears changed nice and smoothly with some nice low ratios. I added these cable stops on the dowtube shifter bosses to neaten things up a bit. They also offer a bit more cable tensioning to adjust the gears.

Brakes

Tektro cantelevers are the way forward as far as I'm concerned on a touring bike. They are about as simple as a brake can be, have loads of clearance for big tires and very little to go wrong with them. The addition of a Textro cable hanger on the front gave a nice smooth action.

Wheels

I deliberated for ages on the these. I had a pair of hand built 32 spoke Hope RS mono hubs with Mavic CXP rims. The hubs have sealed bearings, which similarly to the other Hope components re not serviceable on the road, but they last for so long that this shouldn't be a problem. Loads of forum posts seemed to suggest some pretty beefy wheels and tires for touring and I was starting to doubt whether they would do the job. I spoke to Tony the wheel builder at Cycle Art who assured me they would be absolutely fine. Given he built my race bike wheels that have seen their fair share of Northumberland potholes and never needed a true I figured I would trust his judgement.

Tyres

Everyone seems to use Schwalbe Marathons, but they seem really heavy to me. I've opted for a 28mm Schwalbe Durano plus. Much lighter, but still pretty good puncture resistance. I've been told I need wider tyres, but I've ridden this bike fully laden on gravel paths and it's been fine.

Racks

I asked the guys at Gyllside what I should get and they gave me Tubus racks. It's a good job I didn't ask the price at the time as I might have had secon thoughts. I'm glad I got them though as they are fantastic. Stee, so repairable, but also light weight due to their tubular construction. And they are rock solid on the bike, absolutely no play or flex in them whatsoever.

Pedals

Shimano XT SPD's.

The bars, stem and seatpost are nothing special, just random components I had lying around from other bikes.

After spending two weeks on this bike about the only thing I will change is the position of the Jtek pulley. I put it right on the rear derailleur, but it pokes up a bit and my rear panier kept knocking it and I would need to adjust the gear tension. The pulley can be placed anywhere on the path of cable so I will probably find a more secure spot for it, maybe somewhere on the downtube.
