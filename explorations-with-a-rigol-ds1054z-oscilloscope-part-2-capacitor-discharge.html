Explorations with a Rigol DS1054Z Oscilloscope Part 2 - Capacitor discharge

Wed 22 April 2020
By jpowcode

In this tutorial we will look at the discharge rate of a capacitor using a Rogol 1054z digital oscilloscope. We are going to study a capacitor that takes a long time to discharge, in the order of 10’s of seconds. The article will be mainly concentrated on the use of the scope rather than providing a lesson in physics. We are going to follow this process.

  1. Select suitable values for a capacitor and resistor.
  2. Set up a circuit with a capacitor in parallel with a resistor to discharge through. Connect this to the scope and a power supply.
  3. Set up the scope
  4. Charge the capacitor.
  5. Observe the capacitor discharging on the scope.
  6. Compare theory with reality.

#. The time constant for a capacitor in parralel with a resistor is given by tau = RC is the time in which the capacitor takes to get to approximately 37% of it’s initial voltage. $R$ is the value of the resistor in ohms. $C$ is the value of the capacitor in Farads. More information here . Ideally we’d like to see the value of $tau$ to be about 10 seconds to give us enough time to observe this on the scope. Selecting values of $C=1000mu F$ and $R=10k$ gives us $/tau = 10/times 1000/times 10^{3} /times 10^{6} = 10s$

  1. Wire up a capacitor in parallel with a resistor. You can do this with crocodille clips and it should look something like the picture below. You could also solder together a circuit with a switch to make it easier. I’ve also shown this in photos below.
  2. Turn your power supply off ( you can use a battery for this if you don’t have a power supply). Connect your power supply to the capacitor making sure the negative of the power supply is connected to the negative of the capacitor. A setting of about 5 Volts on the power supply is sufficient. When you turn the power supply on it will charge the capacitor. A few seconds is fine to fully charge the capacitor. When you turn the power supply off the capacitor will discharge through the resistor and this is what we will be measuring.
  1. I’ll be referring to the figures below when explaining this step
    1. Turn scope on (fig 1 - 10)
    2. Connect a probe to channel 1 (fig 1 - 13) and set switch on the probe to 1X
    3. Press the channel 1 button on the scope (fig 2 top left) Your screen should now look something like this. The menu on the right is the important part for now. To change the menu item you press the button next to it (fig 1 -12) twist the dial (fig 1 - 3, fig 3) and press the dial to select it. Make sure your values match those in pic - 1.
    4. We are now going to set the vertical axis on the scope which measures voltage. At the very bottom left of the screen (fig 5 -12) you should see a voltage value displayed next to the number 1. This shows the volts per division (the small squares on the screen). We are going to charge the capacitor to 5v. There are 8 divisions on the screen so if we set the volts per division to 1v we should have plenty of space to see the graph. You can do this by twisting the dial (fig 1 - 13, fig 2 bottom right)
    5. If you look at the top left of the screen (fig 5 - 4) you should see a time measurement which the time per horizonatl division on the screen. There are 12 horizontal divisions. If we set the number of seconds per division to 5s this will give a minute to observe the capacitor discharge. To do this turn the dial (fig 1 - 16, fig 4 bottom)
    6. If you look at the top of the screen to the right of the time division (fig 5 - 5) you should see a value with units MSa/s. This is the number of samples per second that the scope will capture. On its current setting the scope will take 2 Million samples per second. This is far too many for our purpose and will led to a very noisy out put. Observe pictures 3a and 3b which show the a high sample rate (3a) and a low sample rate 3b). To change the sample rate press the Aquire button (fig 1 - 4) your screen should look something like this. Notice that we can’t set the sample rate directly but we have to set the total number of samples. Using the buttons to the right of the menu and the dial (fig 1 - 3) set this to it’s lowest value.
    7. Trigger? Press Run
    1. You now need to connect the scope probe to the resistor. Make sure the negative of the probe (the little wire with the crocodile clip attached) is attached to the end of the resistor that is connected to the negative of the capacitor. Clip the other end of the scope probe by pulling back on the plastic disk to reveal the the metal prong to the other end of the resistor.
    2. Now turn your power supply on briefly, a few seconds should do. To observe the voltage value more directly you can also connect a multimetre to the same place as the scope probe (pic - 3)
  3. At first you won’t see anything on the scope screen. This is perfectly normal, it could take a few tens of seconds before you see anything. When the scope has collected enough data you will begin to see the trace. Just before the trace reaches the right hand edge of the screen press the stop button (fig 1 -7). If you don’t do this you will loose the data for the first part of the trace.
  4. You are now going to use the cursor functionality to calcualate the time constant for the capacitor, resistor circuit. a) Press the cursor button (fig 1 - 4). Your screen should look something like pic - 4. b) Select manual cursor mode by using the dial (fig 1 - 3) press to select. c) You now have access to four cursors. Two in the X direction, AX and BX and two in the Y direction AY and BY. Press the dial (fig 1 - 3) to alternate between them and turn the dial to adjust them. You are trying to get the lower Y cursor to be 37% of the upper Y cursor. Then alter the X cursors to read of the time values. When you are done your screen should look something like pic -5. d) The difference between the time values can be read off the screen as BY - AY. Compare this with the time constant for the circuit which should be 10s.
